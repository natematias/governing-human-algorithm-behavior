# COMM 4940 Final Project 

The final project for this class is an intervention into academic and/or policy conversations on human-algorithm research. In 2026, the class will support projects in two areas:

- An in-depth case study and/or forensic analysis of some alleged AI system failure (see the AI Incident Database for ideas)
- A proposal for a "black box" or data schema of what to record to inform future analysis of incidents
- A research project of your own choosing, if your team includes a PhD student

All final projects will involve three parts:
- a joint presentation of your project
- a joint project submission of some kind that includes a significant written component but is not limited to writing
- a 500-800 word personal reflection on one aspect of the project you learned from or want to think more about.

# Requirements for a final project

- It needs to be empirical - you need to work with evidence, or at least propose how people can work with evidence
- It needs to be about an AI failure of some kind- what you do *after* something bad happens to investigate individual cases or record and aggregate many cases for analysis
- It needs to have an accompanying paper that makes an argument

# Final Project examples
Here are examples of the structure of final projects. I have chosen examples that are unrelated to student interests this year, in order to offer illustrative examples that I do not expect you to take up.

## Example A: investigating self driving car crashes:
- A proposal for ways to improve the NHTSA incident reporting system for self-driving car crashes
- This proposal is supported by an investigation into a specific car crash that the NHTSA has published. To do this, students analze that car crash, visit the scene, look up news reports, read the NHTSA report, and then think about what is left out of the story.
- The final paper has an outline like this:
  - The NHTSA tries to record self driving car crashes in order to understand the causes of failures and how to fix them
  - Here is a specific crash (describe in detail) and what is included/left out of the NHTSA report
  - If we really wanted to understand what happened, we would add X, Y, and Z to the standard

## Example B: investigating the use of AI in plagiarism cases
- A proposal for what evidence Cornell should seek to incorporate into investigations of AI plagiarism (and perhaps what it should not)
- This proposal is supported by an investigation into a specific plagiarism case. This is challenging because the involved parties might not want to contribute to the process, but you might find a few folks. Another option would be to interview people involved in those cases, and find out what
- The final contribution might include:
  - A policy document recommending standards of evidence in AI plagiarism cases
  - A paper that:
    - Introduces the problem of AI plagiarism and the concerns on both student and university sides
    - Summarizes the evidentiary challenges in such cases
    - Summarizes the current state of evidence used in such cases
    - Makes recommendations, and explains the policy document

## Example C: a data schema for reporting content moderation failures
- An actual database design, with some examples, for reporting content moderation failures to the European Union DSA (which doesn't record them to my knowledge)
- The database schema is supported by an investigation into one or more specific content moderation failures, which you review in depth. The final contribution would include:
  - The data schema
  - A paper that:
    - Explains what an AI content moderation failure is and why we need to know what they are
    - Explains the shortcomings of a transparency system reliant on AI content moderation that doesn't record AI failures or decisions that are overruled (I think it doesn't?)
    - Summarizes the challenge of privacy related to AI transparency
    - Proposes a data schema for recording AI content moderation failures that produces useful knowledge without releasing private information

# Final project proposal presentation
The purpose of the proposal presentation is to start thinking about a final project and gain an opportunity for feedback. 

What goes into a good proposal presentation:
- Title page
- Introduce yourselves
- Introduce a story of an individual failure 
- One slide that summarizes things we might need to know
- One slide that summarizes what information already exists
- A big idea
- How you will make progress on that big idea
- What you need in order to pull it off
- Timeline
- Open questions this is the most important

It's okay if you don't know exactly where you're going to get the data - the proposal is not graded and is a starting point that we can work from.
